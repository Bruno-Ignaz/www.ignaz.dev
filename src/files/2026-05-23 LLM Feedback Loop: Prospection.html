LLM Feedback Loop: a Prospection

23th May, 2026; Bruno I. R.

Even though it might not sound like a thought experiment, it is a thought experiment.

Not having any valuable skills has social and economic consequences. Skill is a collection of related patterns. Today, a skill is as easy to automate as you can collect the relevant data at scale. And it seems not impossible that the amount of data required for automation becomes smaller in the future.

While past computer models only predicted specific mathematically well understood patterns, today's AI models are able to produce patterns as complicated as conversations and software after undergoing a computational expensive and data intensive training process. LLM's, like ChatGPT, are one kind of AI model specialized for predicting the next word. After finishing the training process, LLM's learning abilities are temporary for the running conversation. It's important to understand that the models can learn new patterns permanently, even after the initial training process finished. Providers are able to iteratively train their models on newly collected data. What data providers are using to improve their models remains unclear, the models keep improving though.

The more patterns a model can reproduce, the more useful it becomes, the more people will benefit from using it, the more data the provider can collect, and the more patterns the model can reproduce closing the loop. After a cold start, the feedback loop becomes self-reinforcing resulting in ever improving models. At some point, the benefits of an ever improving model might diminish, but nobody can predict whether and when it happens.

Because of this feedback loop, individuals want to learn valuable skills that ideally remain impossible to reproduce for AI models. Yet, LLM's make the vast amounts of language patterns accessible by being helpful to the individual by answering questions. Even though users of LLM's do leak all shared language patterns with the LLM's provider, everybody is using them. In the long-term, LLMs seem to effectively resolve knowledge silos as all patterns are slowly added to the training data. Therefore, the ideal skill must be resistant to data leakage because eventually enough patterns will be leaked. No valuable skill is resistant to pattern leakage. The only skill that is completely resistant is the skill without any patterns and that skill is hardly valuable at all. This is a trivial but interesting conclusion from our definition of skill. How high is the resistance of your skill to data leakage?

The more distributed a pattern is in society, the more data will be leaked to the provider, the faster the LLMs will catch up to it. Individuals are already weighting up the risk of sharing patterns against the benefit the LLM provides to them. No one is willing to give any data freely. But individuals drive LLM progress nevertheless. Individuals leak their LLM usage patterns for ever-growing time horizons. Now we have a race between the capabilities of individuals to produce visions and LLMs learning to replicate them. Do current LLMs need years, decades or centuries to catch up? For the next few years LLM time horizons will be observed very carefully by many as this has implications for the future.

Patterns about the situational context (like constraints, goals, preferences) remain in the hand of the individual for now as companies have limited access to the personal environment. Phones, home security devices, personal assistants, personal robots or brain computer interfaces are going to make the personal environment more accessible. As long as AI requires compute clusters to run on, models will remain in the cloud and data collection will be limited to some degree by the network infrastructure. But even compute clusters have limits that could slow down or prevent progress at some point. Either way, progress in compute hardware is and will remain a hot but currently stagnating topic.

The edge of the individual will be less about secrets, instead it will be about the ability to deal with situations where machines fail, the ability to define long-term visions, and the ability to execute this vision in an ever-changing environment. LLMs are prone to failure in many ways. They are built on top of the assumption that previous observations predict future behavior. They learn patterns empirically, making them to favor frequent patterns. To learn at all, LLMs need hundreds if not thousands of examples to learn. In situations with unclear context (constraints, goals, preferences) bad decisions can easily accumulate.

A race has started and failure is a requirement for LLMs to progress. Nobody knows if and how fast LLMs catch up. It's likely that not all providers will survive the competition, and it's likely that not all skills will remain valuable. Data and compute are certainly limiting factors for LLM progress. Individuals are able to handle machine failures, to define goals and visions, and are able to deal with a changing environment. But the LLM feedback loop makes patterns accessible to everybody (with a subscription) in the long-term.
